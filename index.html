Why CAIS?

The problem with averages

A batter scoring 35 off 25 balls against a tailender in a dead middle over looks identical to 35 off 25 against a pace ace in the powerplay with wickets down. Conventional strike rate and average flatten all of that context. CAIS does not.

The metric is built on ball-by-ball data from every major T20 competition. Every delivery is re-priced before it hits the tally.

How CAIS is calculated

The full formula, every multiplier, every weight.

Batting CAIS

Each ball a batter faces is worth its raw run value multiplied by three context weights:

CAISbat = Σ(runs × phasebat × pressure × stage × opponent) ÷ balls × 100 × formavg
FactorValuesWhy
phasebat PP: 0.95 · Mid: 1.15 · Death: 1.35 Scoring in the powerplay (field up, new ball, two-out fielders) is the easiest phase — the death (yorkers, slower balls, max spread) is the hardest.
pressure 1.0 → 1.5 Combined: wicket-loss pressure × chase pressure. Rises with wickets already lost AND, in the 2nd innings, with required run rate.
formavg 1.0 → 1.45 Rolling 3-match run window. Runs on a purple patch score a little extra.
stage Group: 1.0 · QF: 1.10 · SF/Q/Elim: 1.20 · Final: 1.30 Runs in the knockouts count more than group-stage runs. The latest match of every (league, season) is the Final; the 1–3 matches in the preceding week are the semis / qualifiers / eliminator. Disabled for men's T20 internationals since bilaterals have no knockout structure.
opponent (WC only) Test vs Test: 1.0 · Test vs Assoc: 0.85 · Assoc vs Test: 1.20 In the T20 World Cup, runs by a Full-Member against a qualified associate (Namibia, Netherlands, USA, etc.) carry less weight; runs by an associate player against a Test side carry more.

Bowling CAIS

Each delivery scores the bowler positively on wickets and negatively on runs conceded, with wicket value scaled by nine factors:

wicket_value = 30 × phase×role × batter_tier × form × pressure × partnership_mult × early_wicket_mult × stage × opponent
run_cost = runs_conceded × phasebowl × 0.5 × stage × opponent
CAISbowl = Σ(wicket_value − run_cost) ÷ overs

The pressure decomposition (v2)

Pressure is no longer a single number. It combines two independent components:

wicket_pressure = clip(1 + 0.1 × (cum_wkts / over × 2 clipped to 3), 1.0, 1.3)
chase_pressure = clip(1 + 0.04 × (required_rr − 8.0), 1.0, 1.3) // 2nd innings only
pressure = clip(wicket_pressure × chase_pressure, 1.0, 1.5)

chase_pressure ramps up once the required run rate climbs above 8 (par T20 pace). At RRR = 10 it's 1.08; at 12 it's 1.16; at 15.5+ it hits the cap of 1.3. First-innings balls get chase_pressure = 1.0, so pressure reduces to wicket_pressure alone.

Design choices

Why is batting the powerplay the easiest? Only two fielders outside the circle, new ball travels fastest off the bat, and bowlers are still dialling in. The metric rewards runs scored where runs are actually hard to come by — middle and death — not the easy phase.

Why does breaking a big partnership matter? A 50-run stand represents ~20% of a defendable total. Removing one of the two batters erases compounding match equity, which has a much larger win-probability impact than dismissing a new batter.

Why reward early wickets? A first-over wicket exposes a fresh batter to the new ball under lights, forces a revised batting plan, and statistically depresses the opposition total by 15–25 runs on average — far more than a middle-over dismissal.

Why cap combined pressure at 1.5? Even the most extreme T20 moment (collapsing chase at 9 down needing 14 an over) shouldn't make a ball count for more than 1.5× its baseline. The cap stops pathological match states from swamping the leaderboard.

Why does stage matter? A century in a dead group game and a century in the final of a knockout are treated identically by conventional stats. They shouldn't be: the final is typically tighter, decided on smaller margins, and played under far more scrutiny. Stage adds +10 % for quarters, +20 % for semis / qualifiers / eliminator, and +30 % for the final itself. Group-stage cricket stays at baseline.

Why does opponent quality apply only at the World Cup? Franchise leagues are roughly peer-matched — every side fields a professional T20 squad. The T20 World Cup is different: its main draw mixes Full-Member nations with qualified associates (Namibia, Netherlands, USA, Scotland, etc.), and the talent gap is real. A Test batter smashing Namibia's 5th bowler is not the same as the same batter taking down Australia's attack, so CAIS discounts the former to 0.85×. The mirror is also true — a Namibian bowler taking an Indian wicket is rewarded 1.20×. Test-vs-Test stays at baseline. All other competitions (including T20 bilaterals, which are already filtered to Full-Member nations) pass through at 1.0.
